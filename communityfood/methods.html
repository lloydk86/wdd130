Preservation Methods

Preservation protects quality, nutrition, and affordability. Start with one method, practice the steps, and prioritize safety at every stage.

Fermentation

Fermentation uses beneficial microbes to transform and stabilize foods. It enhances flavor, preserves nutrients, and adds gut‑friendly benefits.

  • Benefits: Deep flavor, improved digestibility, natural preservation without heat.
  • Steps: Wash produce, mix salt brine, pack jars, submerge solids, ferment at room temperature, refrigerate when ready.
  • Safety: Keep everything clean, avoid mold by maintaining submersion, discard batches with off smells.

Drying

Drying removes moisture to prevent spoilage. It’s energy‑efficient, space‑saving, and ideal for fruits, vegetables, herbs, and chilies.

  • Benefits: Long shelf life, lightweight storage, concentrated flavor, simple tools (sun, oven, dehydrator).
  • Steps: Wash and slice evenly, arrange on racks, dry at low heat with airflow, test dryness, store airtight.
  • Safety: Fully dry to prevent mold, cool before sealing, label with date and contents.

Canning

Canning heat‑treats food in sealed jars to prevent spoilage. Use water‑bath canning for high‑acid foods and pressure canning for low‑acid foods.

  • Benefits: Shelf‑stable meals, consistent quality, convenient storage without refrigeration.
  • Steps: Sterilize jars, fill with food, wipe rims, apply lids, process in boiling water or pressure canner, cool, check seals.
  • Safety: Match food type to method, follow tested recipes and times, never use jars with failed seals.
  1. Cleanliness: Wash hands, tools, and surfaces before every step.
  2. Accurate methods: Match food type to the correct preservation method.
  3. Temperature and time: Follow reliable guidelines; do not shorten processing.
  4. Submersion and sealing: Keep fermenting solids under brine; confirm canning seals before storage.
  5. Labeling and storage: Date every jar, note method, and store in cool, dark places.
